Yesterday, I tweeted something general, though generalized from within a specific situational context for me. The tweet said:
Once again, knowing (good or bad) is so much better than not knowing.
Pretty quickly, Jon Bach (@jbtestpilot) replied with the following:
@dckismet Good blog idea. Now go blog it so I can disagree. :)
Welllllll, ooooooookaaaaaaaaaaaaayyyyyyyyyyyyy, but oh boy, what's coming? I'm feeling a little like a ninja, making sure I prevent any attack before it can happen :)
In any case, what I eventually settled on was the fact that there must be a set of circumstances under which what I said holds true (this is the case I was making in my tweet), and a set of circumstances under which it does not hold true. I hadn't been able to think of many obvious situations where I wouldn't have agreed with that statement, so I had tweeted it as the generalized statement I made.

Let me just qualify for a minute how I feel about generalizations -- I'm not a terrible fan of them. I usually am teased for the depth with which I qualify just about everything I say, especially in my professional life as a tester. Unfortunately, the early part of my career involved jobs where I was asked if a release was ready to go and I said "Yes", only to have issues crop up and have my good name sullied in the wake of failure. I learned quickly to say things like "Well, we are aware of x high priority issues and y lower priority issues. Given what I know, I would be comfortable letting the customer have it." In short, I generally don't generalize. 140 characters makes that difficult.

Conveniently, I am not at liberty to divulge the exact situation that lead me to my tweet yesterday, but what I can do is describe what types of situations my statement fits for. Certainly, I can think of examples where finally knowing something that was previously uncertain is "better" -- usually for me, "better" means that I can have enough certainty about the situation to be comfortable making decisions about moving forward.

Generally speaking, I think that "knowing" (I'll just abbreviate the entire statement with that one word) is better when something is happening that impacts future activity.
